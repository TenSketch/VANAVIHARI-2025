Amruthadhara Falls

Amruthadhara Falls is a seasonal waterfall located in the East Godavari district of Andhra Pradesh, India. It is a popular destination for nature lovers, especially during the monsoon season when the falls are at their most spectacular. The falls are formed by the confluence of two streams, the Varaha and the Penna. They cascade down a series of rocky cliffs, creating a mesmerizing sight. The falls are surrounded by lush greenery, making for a truly picturesque setting.

Rampa Falls

In the semi-evergreen forests of the Agency track in East Godavari District, numerous picturesque spots and perennial waterfalls grace the landscape. Among them is Rampa V.S.S, surrounded by high forests showcasing Neelakanteswara and Rampa waterfalls. Sri Neelakanteswara Vana Vihara Sthali is located approximately 4 km from the village of Rampachodavaram in East Godavari District.

Gudisa View Point, situated in the Gudisa hill range of Andhra Pradesh, offers breathtaking panoramic views of the Eastern Ghats. This grassland area is renowned for its lush green valleys, dense forests, and mist-covered hills, making it a haven for nature enthusiasts and photographers. The region is accessible via a challenging road, with the last 12 kilometers featuring the Gudisa Ghat Road, known for its sharp bends and unpaved paths. Visitors can enjoy activities such as trekking and bird watching.

The Gudisa grassland forests of ASR District have rich biodiversity and semi-evergreen forests with undulating terrain, forming part of the Eastern Ghats. The journey to the Gudisa grasslands includes many streams flowing over undulating rocks in the deep woods, giving visitors a thrilling experience in nature. The Gudisa grasslands have specific characteristics of open grasslands with up to a 10-kilometer view. Especially at sunrise and sunset, the location is very beautiful.

The Gudisa grasslands are located in the Maredumilli Range of Rampachodavaram Division Reserve Forests, a high hill station. These open grasslands have sparse, stunted tree and shrub growth exclusively on hilltops above +1235 meters altitude along the Eastern Ghats. It is an interesting vegetation type due to its unusual characteristics. Though it is part of the dry deciduous forests, it is a climatic climax type of vegetation of the Eastern Ghats. Additionally, tourism activities are regulated to preserve the ecosystem, and overnight stays are prohibited to prevent ecological damage and disturbances to local tribes. Several species here are on the RET (Rare, Endangered, and Threatened) list, and three are endemic species unique to the region.

Located within the Eastern Ghats, Gudisa Hill Station not only captivates with its natural beauty but also stands as a centre of cultural and ecological significance. It is home to the Konda Reddy tribe, where visitors can witness preserved tribal arts, crafts, and traditional knowledge. The region offers enriching experiences such as nature education, medicinal plant awareness, and sustainable tourism practices. Guided local tours provide access to trekking trails, scenic viewpoints, and birdwatching spots, including sightings of the rare Green Munia.
